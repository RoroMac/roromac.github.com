Final Project Academic Research


The internet is indisputably a large mechanism, with millions of webpages. Google is the most widely-used program for indexing this information through relevant keywords. However, this essay serves as a reference to explore how we find new ideas. Especially, how do we find relevant information, tools, programs, or associations, especially if we don’t know the words? Do we rely on news articles, forums, or help columns to pose our questions and hope that someone recommends a resource suited for our needs?

Columbia Law School professor Tim Wu, assesses the virtual economy that’s composed of digital monopolies in his published 2012 Wall Street Journal article,“In the Grip of the New Monopolists; Do away with Google? Break up Facebook? We can’t imagine life without them— and that’s the problem”. He begins his article by assessing the cultural impact which these titans have and their claim on basic functions. Users go to these companies because they’re simply the most convenient. “Google ‘owns’ search; Facebook, social networking; eBay rules auctions….”(Wu). Wu makes a critical stance when he claims that it’s not a bad thing that these information is dictated by these large corporations and that it’s natural. Monopolies grow from technical superiority. However, their attempt to stay relevant in transition periods of another company surpassing is when their user-base often faces troubles. Additionally, he warns readers about the dangers of monopolies and their usual resistance to change and lack of innovation.

Adrianne Jeffries is a writer and editor about articles intersecting between technology and science. Her post “Once the biggest source of social traffic on the web, StumbleUpon fights to stay relevant” on a digital news site, The Verge, talks about decline of one of the biggest social media companies nearly a decade ago. StumbleUpon is a random website indexer. Users can click a button and be directed to one of its many randomly suggested websites. Users can either like or dislike the website and continue on their virtual journey with user-generated content. In 2009, StumbleUpon was the most popular social media website with 25 million users and a button pushed roughly 1.2 million times per month. To keep up with modern graphic styles and compete with other growing social media apps, StumbleUpon redesigned their website in 2012. However, this redesign included features that seemed unnecessary, didn’t work, and “seemed lonely” (Jeffries) in comparison to the discovery websites like Reddit or Digg. Additionally, it seemed like StumbleUpon had an ineffective model for advertisements. Jeffries makes a claim that while StumbleUpon (at the time) was a useful tool for finding new information, it was effectively a diversion. Ultimately, it competed with any social media or recreational activities. It appears that they weren’t able to fully compete after shutting down in 2018.

To understand how people use the internet, I looked towards metaphors. Josh Dzieza, a technology-interest writer at The Verge, writes about the cognitive importance these metaphors in “A history of metaphors for the internet”. He makes a claim that metaphors have a way of humanizing and understanding the very difficult concept of the internet. The cyberspace was coined as a method to describe leaving your body and encountering a whole wide arrange of information. The information highway was changed this idea to depict the internet as a space that takes users to wherever they want. However, this connotation has also been used as an argument for government regulations to an argument for virtual freedom. Could we apply the idea of metaphors for changing how we perceive current search engines?
