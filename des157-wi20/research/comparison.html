Interaction Design Comparative Research

Without a doubt, Google has been one of the largest developments to originate from the dotcom bubble. It has become a cultural icon for its ability to catalogue virtual information in a manner so that anyone, regardless of demographic, can find anything. A consequence for creating a tool meant for finding other objects is that people hardly pay attention to the apparatus itself. This may be accredited to the very easy to use user interface. However, in their many subdivisions it’s surprising to see how the user interface changes.

Google.com

Google search engine is its original function. It has the ability to take in a couple of selected keywords to generate results it deems relevant to the users’ search.

The main page is simple. “Google” is centered and colorful, directing the users eye. A search bar underneath acts like an underline. Beneath it are the two functions; “Google Search” which generates a list of results and “I’m Feeling Lucky” which directs users to the most relevant result. The s implicity makes it easy. Users aren’t overwhelmed with a barrage of special effects and distracting animations. The magnifying glass is used to further reinforce what users can do with the bar and indicate it as an element that users can work with. A microphone, is shown in a matching color palette to indicate an interactive element and an on-hover dropdown tells users that they can talk into the search bar for further accessibility.

Located around the edges of the page are small words and icons. These icons take you to other functions of google, but are often overlooked on the main page because of their miniscule presence. On the right side are redirect links for products meant which users can use. “Images” and “Gmail” are explicitly listed while the others are hidden behind a grid icon. The left side, are more informational links to show what Google can do.

After entering an item into the search bar, a list of relevant information is shown. The top bar stays relatively similar between search results. The Google icon in the top left corner redirects to the main page (although often unnecessary to search again). The search bar is still present so users can enter new vocabulary for another search. More specialized search tools are presented underneath the bar so that users can immediately select what kind of information they want presented; gis, webpages, news, images, etc. The information itself is typically presented in a vertical with webpages and a short description where the term can be found. Google also predicts what kind of information the user wants by including translations, definitions, questions asked about, and more. Searches related by Google allows users to find more information without having to enter more keywords. There is an abundance of whitespace used on both sides (more on the right), a lack of color (with the exception of included images), and very few variations to make the program easy to use. Google search also limits the amount of information that users see per page to make it less overwhelming. An additional panel for relevant searches are placed above the page directory so that users can simply generate a new search with more specific terms than go to the next page.

About.Google

The “About Google” page is surprisingly different and hard to navigate. Users are greeted with a mission statement in large big letters that match the Google color palette. Users can either scroll down or use a hamburger navigation menu to find what they’re looking for. The main elements are divided into stories, commitment, and products. The main page also links to company hiring and archived google doodles. The footer contains an even more expansive list of options in categories ranging in press, company, terms of service, and their non-profit work. The website uses blue to indicate interactive (clickable) areas. The pictures are large, colorful, and in high quality. The contents of its page are separated by a gracious amount of white space.

A Black History Month section is placed at the top for its relevance. Clicking it leads to a web page that appears to have been designed a little bit more thoughtfully. Images decorate the background in relevance to the theme and an in-page navigation bar is used at the top to redirect users to special topics. Additionally, a video is placed at the very top to act as a prelude to the information below. This video doesn’t autoplay and its duration is listed on top of it. The first section, history makers, integrates scrolling through an animation. Based on its position, a name, description, and position come up based on its category. The phrase “the most searched...” is a clever way to integrate their corporate identity as a search company. The rest of the webpage falls in line with its identity as it uses large font-sizes, images, and lots of white space.

Another web page of the site is their stories section where it includes narratives of how people are using Google-Sponsored technology to make impact. Halfway through, there is a video that plays and surprises the users by changing the entire web page into dark mode to focus further on the video. Beneath that video, a scrolling line shows recently published videos. If a title catches the users’ eye, they can put their mouse on the line to stop the moving line and can click on that video. Another interesting interface is the use of a grid that has a dropdown menu so that users can change what information it provides. However, the interface is oddly very distinct from the rest of the webpage and does not integrate well.

The interesting thing about this website is how it very subtly redirects users into other websites also owned by google. By going into the Google commitments tab users can be redirected to Safety.Google, Sustainability.Google, or Ai.google. These websites all look remarkably similar to them but there’s so many small different variations that it’s confusing and an eyesore to look at. My favorite atrocity being the sustainability.google and yourplanyourplanet.sustainability.google which disconnected from each other. And because these are all different websites, users are confused by why the navigation changes for seemingly no reason between pages.

Granted that there are a lot of interesting user interface tools being used between web pages, it’s hard to remember where they lie in the confusing Google hierarchy of information.

Chrome Music Lab

The chrome music lab is a Google experimental website that is nested deep within experiments.withgoogle.com. This series of thirteen different interactive animations allow users to learn more about music through helpful visuals. Icons are separated as a grid holding different welcoming and colorful visuals. Upon mouse hover, the title of the exercise appear with an arrow to reinforce the let’s go idea. Beneath the grid, is an explanation with the signature use of excessive white space and colors to indicate hyperlinks. A navigation bar is featured at the top to direct to the experiments or the about section. A link to Google is involved at the bottom (but not the google experiment page). Two programs that I thought were very interesting were Kandinsky and Piano Roll. Both create sounds through visuals, but in very distinct methods.

Kandinsky is an empty white board. Users hold down the mouse and drag it to create a mark which is then translated into a particular sound. This experiment teaches children, Kandinsky’s philosophy of transitioning visuals into sounds. However, there are only a couple of colors used and very few sound distinctions. It is interesting though, that if you draw a circle then a face appears to make a more humanistic choir-sound. When the user presses play, the canvas is read as a musical note from left to right. Only two different colors are used in the piece and three color combinations can be determined.

Another experiment that I enjoyed playing with was the piano roll. Common music pieces are played while a visual runs on screen. Musical notes are distinguished by color and at various heights. Users can choose which musical pieces they want to play and how the notes sound (piano, or electronic synthesizer). Another interesting thing about it, is how users can record a sound and listen to how Google autotunes it to match the pitch.

CommonSans

A free font developed by a Swedish design firm Essen International and nonprofit Solvatten. There isn’t too much in terms of interaction, but it implements a subtle but powerful story of reminding people that refugees are people too. It is a nice and well-designed sans-serif font. It also proposes a question of if implemented with the services of Google could it help promote good behavior or could it be seen as limitation of free speech and met with criticism. Additionally, this font can only seem to recognize three types of spellings Refugee, refugee, and REFUGEE. Any other combination of capitals and lower-case letters are undetected.
